## A Correction on the Lineage, and the Larger Map It Opens

The companion README on the Polanyi–Wigner–von Neumann lineage established the correct genealogy: László Rátz taught both Wigner and von Neumann at Budapest's Fasori Gymnasium; Michael Polanyi supervised Wigner's doctoral thesis; and Wigner and Polanyi maintained a documented decade-long correspondence on tacit knowledge and the quantum measurement problem. Wigner was not von Neumann's teacher. They were co-students of the same extraordinary pedagogue, and lifelong colleagues at the Princeton Institute for Advanced Study.

That lineage is one thread. The present README traces the loom.

The Budapest–Berlin–Princeton axis was not an isolated phenomenon. It was the most luminous concentration within a far larger intellectual field — a field in which the same historical crisis generated, simultaneously and in proximity, two mirror-image responses to the same question. The question was: *what is the structure of knowledge, and what are its limits?* One response said: knowledge is formalizable — build the axioms, build the machines, build the systems that process explicit information at scale. The other said: knowledge has an irreducible personal, embodied, and social dimension — no formal system, however powerful, can hold what knowing most essentially is.

Both responses were right. Together, they constitute the complete epistemological map of what machine intelligence can and cannot do. The *Attentional Arbitrage* framework inherits both. What it has not yet named is the larger intellectual history that both traditions share: the same cities, the same catastrophe, the same exile, and — remarkably — the same decade of crystallization.

---

## I. The Crisis: What Both Traditions Were Answering

Between 1900 and 1930, the intellectual certainties of Western civilization collapsed in rapid succession. Non-Euclidean geometry had already dissolved the claim that Euclidean space was the necessary form of physical reality. Einstein's special and general relativity had dissolved the Newtonian absolutes of space, time, and simultaneity. Quantum mechanics — Heisenberg's uncertainty principle (1927), Bohr's complementarity, Dirac's formalism — had dissolved the classical picture of a determinate, observer-independent physical world. And set theory's paradoxes, culminating in Russell's paradox (1901) and the crisis of mathematical foundations, had dissolved the assumption that mathematics itself was standing on firm ground.

The philosophical response to this crisis was urgent, and it bifurcated.

**The Formalization Response** — associated with Vienna, Cambridge, and Berlin — doubled down on rigor. If the foundations of knowledge were unstable, they should be rebuilt: explicitly, axiomatically, so that every claim could be derived from stated premises and every derivation could be checked. The Vienna Circle (Schlick, Carnap, Neurath, 1920s–1930s) formulated logical positivism: only propositions verifiable by observation or derivable by logic are meaningful. Carnap's *Logical Structure of the World* (1928) attempted to construct the entire conceptual framework of science from a minimal base of logical and observational primitives. David Hilbert had issued the challenge that catalyzed this program: prove that mathematics is complete (every true statement is provable), consistent (no contradiction is derivable), and decidable (there exists a procedure to determine the truth of any statement). The goal was total formalization — the elimination of any residue of the informal, the personal, the tacit.

**The Tacit Response** — associated with Budapest, Manchester, Oxford, and later Chicago — went the other way. The crisis of foundations was not a defect of current formal systems but a revelation about the nature of knowledge itself. Knowing is not axiom-following. It is a personal, embodied, historically situated practice whose deepest dimension cannot be articulated without being destroyed. The appropriate response to the crisis is not more formalization but epistemological honesty about what formalization can and cannot contain. Polanyi's *Personal Knowledge* (1958) was the most comprehensive statement of this response. But the response had been developing across multiple disciplines for decades before Polanyi named it.

The central drama of the intellectual century between 1920 and 1960 is the collision of these two responses — the formalizers and the tacit-knowers — in the same cities, the same buildings, often the same conversations.

## II. The Formalization Tradition: Vienna → Cambridge → Bell Labs → Princeton

The Formalization Response has a canonical genealogy. Frege's *Begriffsschrift* (1879) invented modern symbolic logic. Russell and Whitehead's *Principia Mathematica* (1910–1913) attempted to derive all mathematics from logical axioms. The Vienna Circle operationalized this program for empirical science. And then — in 1931, the year the edifice should have been completed — it was destroyed from within.

Kurt Gödel (1906–1978), a young Austrian logician attending the Vienna Circle's seminars, published his incompleteness theorems. In any consistent formal system rich enough to express arithmetic, there will always be statements that are true but unprovable within the system. The goal of Hilbert's program — complete, consistent, decidable formalization of all mathematics — was mathematically impossible. Gödel showed that if a formal system meets the required conditions, it will contain true claims that it cannot prove.

The philosophical implications were seismic, and their bearing on the question of machine intelligence has never been exhausted. Gödel's theorems demonstrated that truths always lie beyond formal proof within any given system. This is not a limitation of particular systems. It is a structural feature of formalization itself. No accumulation of axioms, no expansion of processing power, no increase in the scale of a formal system can eliminate the gap between what is true and what is provable within that system. Gödel proved, from within the Formalization tradition's own framework, what Polanyi would argue from outside it: that formal systems have constitutive limits that no technical advance can overcome.

Gödel was at Princeton from 1940 until his death in 1978. Von Neumann was at Princeton from 1933. They were colleagues and, by most accounts, mutual admirers — two Austro-Hungarian intellectuals, products of the same Central European mathematical culture, working in adjacent buildings on questions whose deepest connection neither fully articulated. Von Neumann heard Gödel present the incompleteness result in 1930 and immediately grasped its significance. His response was characteristically pragmatic: if the formal program is limited in principle, build what can be built. The stored-program computer, the von Neumann architecture (1945), is the crystallization of the Formalization tradition's constructive program — not a proof of formalization's completeness, but the most powerful formal machine ever built.

Alan Turing (1912–1954), working at Cambridge and Bletchley, provided the theoretical foundation for von Neumann's machine. *On Computable Numbers* (1936) defined the universal Turing machine — a formal device capable of computing any function that can be computed by any algorithm. Turing's formalism specified, simultaneously, what computation is and what it cannot do: the halting problem establishes that no algorithm can determine, for all inputs, whether a given computation will terminate. Claude Shannon (1916–2001), at Bell Labs, completed the information-theoretic picture: *A Mathematical Theory of Communication* (1948) defined information as the reduction of uncertainty, quantifying it in a way that made it formally tractable, transmissible, and processable. Shannon information is exactly the kind of knowledge that the Formalization tradition can handle — explicit, quantifiable, codifiable.

The Formalization tradition, in summary, produced: modern logic (Frege, Russell), the proof of its own limits (Gödel), the universal formal machine (Turing), information theory (Shannon), and the actual machine (von Neumann). It is the intellectual lineage of every AI system ever built.

## III. The Tacit Tradition: Budapest → Berlin → Manchester → Oxford → Chicago

The Tacit Response developed across the same decades, in the same cities, with the same urgency. Its figures knew each other. Many of them knew the Formalizers.

Gestalt psychology — Wertheimer, Köhler, and Koffka, working in Berlin in the 1910s–1920s — established empirically what Polanyi would argue philosophically: the whole is not reducible to its parts, and perception is not the summation of atomic sensory inputs. The Gestalt psychologists demonstrated that human perception grasps structure — figure-ground relationships, patterns of movement, meaningful wholes — that no analysis of the constituent elements can recover. The perceiving mind brings something to the data that is not in the data. This is tacit knowing as experimental result, a decade before Polanyi named it.

Edmund Husserl's phenomenology (Freiburg, 1900s–1930s) contributed the philosophical framework. Husserl's concept of the *Lebenswelt* — the lifeworld, the pre-theoretical background of shared meanings and practices against which all explicit inquiry takes place — is the epistemological ground of the Tacit tradition. Knowing, for Husserl, is always "intentional" — directed toward something — and the intentional structure of consciousness cannot be captured by any formal description of its inputs and outputs. The phenomenological reduction (the *epoché*) reveals that what we take as the "objective" world is always already constituted by a structure of meanings that formal science presupposes but cannot explain. Husserl's final work, *The Crisis of European Sciences* (1936), written as Nazism consolidated its grip on Germany, named the catastrophe he saw coming: the "mathematization of nature," the mistaking of the formal model for reality itself, was producing a civilization that had lost its grip on the Lebenswelt that made science possible in the first place. The *Attentional Arbitrage* companion README's deployment of Habermas's "colonization of the lifeworld" is a direct inheritance of Husserl's diagnosis.

F. A. Hayek (1899–1992) — an Austrian economist, student of the Vienna milieu, and contemporary of the Circle whose program he explicitly rejected — published "The Use of Knowledge in Society" in *The American Economic Review* in 1945. Hayek's central insight was that the knowledge required for economic coordination is not the kind of knowledge that can be centralized. It is dispersed across millions of individuals, embedded in particular circumstances of time and place, largely tacit, and often impossible to articulate even by those who possess it. Hayek was not using Polanyi's vocabulary — *Personal Knowledge* was still thirteen years away — but the argument is the same argument, applied to economics rather than epistemology. The price system functions not because it formalizes and centralizes knowledge but because it aggregates and communicates knowledge that can never be formalized or centralized. The core economic problem of society lies not in allocating known scarce resources but in coordinating the dispersed, partial, and often tacit knowledge held by individuals, which no single authority can fully comprehend or centralize.

Hayek's argument is the economic application of Polanyi's epistemology, published thirteen years before *Personal Knowledge*, reaching the same conclusion from the policy debate over central planning. The Soviet Union's attempt to centralize economic calculation was, for Hayek, an epistemological impossibility — not just a practical difficulty but a structural one, because the knowledge required for rational allocation exists only in the distributed tacit knowing of millions of individual agents and cannot survive the extraction into a central information system. The parallel to AI capability limits is exact: an AI system attempting to perform the cognitive functions that require collective tacit knowledge faces the same impossibility that Hayek identified in the Soviet planning debate. The knowledge is not merely hard to collect. It ceases to exist when extracted from the social practices that constitute it.

## IV. Ludwig Wittgenstein: The One Career That Performed Both Traditions

No figure in the intellectual history of the twentieth century dramatizes the Formalization–Tacit bifurcation more sharply than Ludwig Wittgenstein (1889–1951), because he enacted both traditions in a single career — in sequence, with the explicit acknowledgment that the second was a refutation of the first.

Wittgenstein's *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* (1921) presented a highly structured, formal approach to language. He believed that language mirrored reality and that words corresponded directly to objects or states of affairs in the world. The *Tractatus* is the purest statement of the Formalization tradition in philosophy of language: language is a logical picture of reality; the structure of meaningful propositions mirrors the structure of facts; what cannot be so pictured cannot be meaningfully said. The Vienna Circle treated the *Tractatus* as their philosophical charter, even though Wittgenstein was already skeptical of their reading.

Then Wittgenstein returned to philosophy in 1929, having given it up for years, and began systematically dismantling everything the *Tractatus* had built. In his *Philosophical Investigations* (1953), published posthumously, he introduced the "use theory of meaning," arguing that the meaning of a word lies not in its reference or correspondence to reality but in its use within various "language-games." Language, he suggested, is an activity embedded in the "forms of life" shared by human beings.

The shift from *Tractatus* to *Philosophical Investigations* is the shift from the Formalization tradition to the Tacit tradition, performed within one career. What Wittgenstein discovered, through his own practice of philosophical analysis, was that the attempt to reduce language to its logical form always left a residue — a "form of life," a background of shared practices, a way of going on — that the formal analysis could not capture and that was necessary for any explicit formulation to mean anything at all. This residue is Polanyi's tacit dimension. Wittgenstein's famous remark, "My spade is turned" — the response to the question "why do you follow the rule this way and not that?" — is the point at which the formal justification runs out and something pre-theoretical takes over: the shared practice, the form of life, the tacit knowing that cannot itself be given a rule.

The *Philosophical Investigations* was published in 1953 — five years before Polanyi's *Personal Knowledge* (1958) and thirteen years before Polanyi's *The Tacit Dimension* (1966). Wittgenstein and Polanyi were not in dialogue; they arrived at structurally identical conclusions from different starting points. This convergence is not coincidence. It is two traditions of careful inquiry, each pushed to its limits, reaching the same bedrock.

## V. Norbert Wiener: The Formalizer Who Saw the Consequence

Norbert Wiener (1894–1964) built the machines and read the warning in them. He is the one figure in this lineage who sat entirely inside the Formalization tradition — he was a mathematician of the highest order, working on stochastic processes, Brownian motion, and the mathematical foundations of communication — and who simultaneously articulated, more clearly than anyone else in 1950, what building those machines would do to human beings.

Wiener never claimed that machines would achieve human-like general intelligence. He was careful to distinguish between specific automated functions and the broader capabilities of the human mind. His *Cybernetics* (1948) formalized the study of control and communication in animals and machines, establishing feedback as the unifying concept. Two years later, *The Human Use of Human Beings* (1950) translated that technical framework into a social prognosis. Wiener warned about the social impact of automation, predicting both labor displacement from machines and the dehumanization of workers treated merely as machine parts. He argued that technology applications should enhance, not diminish, human dignity.

Wiener's precise formulation — that any use of a human being that demands less than their full stature is a degradation and a waste — is the strongest statement in the literature of the *Attentional Arbitrage* framework's normative claim, written in 1950. It precedes Ide's formalization (2026) by 76 years. It precedes Peterson's "training for obsolescence" argument (2025) by 75. And it precedes Sen's capability approach (1999) by 49 years, though it reaches the same conclusion: what is destroyed by the mispricing is not return on investment but human stature.

What Wiener could not do was explain, at the epistemological level, why human beings have a "full stature" that exceeds what machines can demand of them. He was a mathematician, not a philosopher of knowledge. The explanation was being assembled contemporaneously — by Polanyi, by Hayek, by Wittgenstein, by Arendt — in adjacent disciplines that Wiener did not synthesize. The framework that the *Attentional Arbitrage* companions construct is, in part, the completion of Wiener's warning: here is the epistemological ground for the normative claim that Wiener stated without grounding.

## VI. Hannah Arendt: The Irreducibility of Action

Hannah Arendt (1906–1975) — a student of Heidegger and Jaspers, a German-Jewish philosopher who fled Nazism through France to New York, where she spent the remainder of her career — published *The Human Condition* in 1958, the same year as Polanyi's *Personal Knowledge* and twelve years after Wittgenstein completed the manuscript that became the *Philosophical Investigations*.

Arendt's contribution to the framework is the most politically urgent. She did not write about knowledge. She wrote about action — the specifically human form of activity that, unlike labor (the cyclical maintenance of biological life) and work (the production of durable objects), introduces genuine novelty into the world. Action is the freest and highest expression of the vita activa because it transcends both the cyclical nature of labor and the means-end, instrumental calculation of work. Through action, we announce who we are. By inserting something "original and unanticipated" into the public sphere, we meaningfully distinguish ourselves from others.

The significance of Arendt's tripartite distinction for the question of machine capability limits is precisely that labor and work are, in principle, automatable — and Arendt understood this when she wrote in 1958, before the computer revolution had produced anything like its present capabilities. Labor, the cyclical repetition of the biological, has always been susceptible to mechanical substitution. Work, the fabrication of artifacts according to a model or plan, is susceptible to algorithmic substitution wherever the model can be specified. Action — the introduction of the genuinely new, the unpredicted, the politically consequential — is not susceptible to substitution because it is constitutively a relation between people, embedded in the condition of plurality (the fact that we share the world with others who are irreducibly different from us), and because it is generative in a way that no specification can anticipate.

The problems Arendt identified — diminishing human agency and political freedom; the paradox that as human powers increase through technological and humanistic inquiry, we are less equipped to control the consequences of our actions — continue to confront us today. The *Attentional Arbitrage* framework's diagnosis of prestige competitions as foreclosing the development of open-world cognitive capability is, in Arendt's vocabulary, a diagnosis of a social system that substitutes labor and work — repetitive, rule-following, metric-optimizing activity — for action, while labeling the substitution as achievement.

Arendt's concept of action maps directly onto the cognitive capabilities the framework identifies as machine-resistant: the capacity for genuine novelty, for judgment in the absence of applicable rules, for the introduction of the unanticipated into a shared world. These are not merely difficult for machines. They are constitutively impossible for machines, because they require the condition of plurality — the fact of being a person among persons, embedded in a shared world, making a claim on others' attention — that no computational system inhabits.

## VII. The 1945–1960 Crystallization: Both Traditions at Once

The two traditions did not develop sequentially. They crystallized simultaneously, in a period of approximately fifteen years, in the aftermath of the catastrophe that had scattered their practitioners.

In 1945, Hayek published "The Use of Knowledge in Society" and von Neumann circulated the First Draft report on the EDVAC. In 1948, Shannon published *A Mathematical Theory of Communication* and Wiener published *Cybernetics*. In 1950, Turing published "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" (posing the Turing Test) and Wiener published *The Human Use of Human Beings*. In 1951, Wittgenstein died, having completed his *Philosophical Investigations*. In 1953, the *Philosophical Investigations* was published. In 1955, Einstein died. In 1957, von Neumann died. In 1958, Polanyi published *Personal Knowledge* and Arendt published *The Human Condition*. In 1960, Wigner published "The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Mathematics" — with its explicit citation of Polanyi. In 1966, Polanyi published *The Tacit Dimension*.

This is not a list of books. It is a map of a conversation: the two traditions, formalizing and tacit, reaching their most concentrated formulations in the same decade, responding to the same crisis, in the same cities and institutions. Princeton housed Einstein, von Neumann, and Gödel. MIT housed Wiener. Chicago housed Hayek and Polanyi. Cambridge housed Wittgenstein and Turing. New York housed Arendt.

The catastrophe — Nazism, totalitarianism, the destruction of the Central European intellectual communities of the 1920s — was the mechanism of both scattering and concentration. It dissolved the communities of practice (the Fasori Gymnasium, the Vienna Circle, the Berlin physics colloquia) that the tacit knowledge tradition was theorizing, and it transported the intellectuals of both traditions to the same American institutions, where the urgency of wartime and postwar reconstruction forced them to their most crystallized formulations. The Manhattan Project, the computing programs, the social theory of mass society and totalitarianism — all were products of exile, urgency, and the forced proximity of people who would not otherwise have occupied the same rooms.

## VIII. The Mirror Structure: What the Two Traditions See in Each Other

The Formalization and Tacit traditions are not opposed. They are mirror-image responses to the same problem, each correct about its own domain, each incomplete without the other.

The Formalization tradition established: formal systems have extraordinary power; all computable functions can be computed by von Neumann machines; Shannon information can be transmitted and processed at arbitrary scale; machine systems can surpass human performance on any task whose structure can be fully specified. Every advance in AI since 1950 confirms this.

The Tacit tradition established: formal systems have constitutive limits (Gödel); tacit knowledge is the ground on which all explicit knowledge rests and cannot be fully extracted from that ground (Polanyi); the price system works precisely because it aggregates knowledge that can never be centralized (Hayek); language is meaningful only within forms of life that no formal system can specify (Wittgenstein); genuine action — the introduction of the genuinely new — requires plurality and cannot be substituted (Arendt); any use of human beings that demands less than their full stature is a degradation (Wiener). The jagged frontier of machine capability (Kapoor et al., 2026) is the empirical confirmation of the tacit tradition's philosophical claims.

The *Attentional Arbitrage* framework is, at its philosophical foundation, the claim that the prestige competition structure has responded to machine capability expansion by valorizing exactly the domain that the Formalization tradition has absorbed — explicit, rule-following, closed-domain cognitive performance — while systematically devaluing the domain that the Tacit tradition identifies as constitutively human. The prestige system is the cultural instantiation of the Formalization tradition's epistemology: the belief that what counts as knowledge is what can be made explicit, measured, ranked, and compared. Correcting it requires not merely new investment advice but a new epistemological settlement — one that grants the Tacit tradition the cultural standing that the Formalization tradition's technological victories have systematically denied it.

## IX. The Framework: The Bifurcation as Analytical Lens

The novel framework this README proposes is the **Bifurcation Model**: the claim that the two intellectual traditions, developed simultaneously in the same milieu, constitute a complete analytical lens for understanding machine capability limits and their cognitive, economic, and civic consequences.

The model has three structural claims.

**First:** The Formalization tradition defines the domain of machine competence. Any cognitive task whose structure is fully specifiable — whose relevant features can be enumerated, whose success conditions can be stated in advance, whose performance can be measured against a closed criterion — is, in principle, absorbable by machine systems of sufficient scale. This domain is larger than most imagined before transformer architectures. It includes much of what credentialing systems have historically valued: closed-domain expertise, rule-following performance, and the manipulation of explicit symbol systems.

**Second:** The Tacit tradition defines the domain of structural machine incapacity. Any cognitive task that requires the embodied, personally situated, socially embedded, historically constituted form of knowing that Polanyi named, that Wittgenstein's forms of life ground, that Hayek's dispersed knowledge requires, that Arendt's action instantiates — is, in principle, unavailable to formal systems. The unavailability is not a technical limitation but a constitutive one: these tasks cannot be specified in the terms that formal processing requires, because their relevant features are not articulable without loss of what makes them the tasks they are.

**Third:** The bifurcation was produced by a historical catastrophe that simultaneously destroyed the communities of practice through which tacit knowledge is transmitted (the Fasori Gymnasium, the Vienna Circle, the Berlin colloquia) and concentrated the thinkers of both traditions in new institutional nodes (Princeton, MIT, Chicago) where the urgency of the moment forced their most consequential formulations. The AI transition is producing a second-order version of this catastrophe: AI-driven entry-level automation is now destroying the communities of practice (Ide, 2026) that the Tacit tradition identified as the mechanism of tacit knowledge transmission, precisely as machine systems are absorbing the explicit-knowledge domain that the Formalization tradition mapped.

The framework does not predict outcomes. It diagnoses the structure of the situation: we are living through the practical consequence of a theoretical bifurcation that was made visible eighty years ago, in the works of people who are now on the required reading lists of elite universities and in the footnotes of AI benchmark papers, but whose convergent argument — about what formal systems can and cannot know — has not yet been synthesized into the cultural and educational responses that the AI transition requires.
